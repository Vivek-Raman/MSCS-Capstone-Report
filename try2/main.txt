\begin{abstract}
Preparing academic documents in \LaTeX\ remains difficult for many researchers and students, not because the system lacks power, but because its syntax places a substantial burden on authors who mainly want to write. General-purpose word processors are easier to learn, yet they often fall short of the precision, reproducibility, and typographic quality expected in academic publishing.
Generative AI introduces a second tension. It promises faster authoring workflows, but scholars remain cautious when tools blur the boundary between assistance and content generation.
Spartan Write responds to both problems by combining an agentic large language model (LLM) with a cross-platform \LaTeX\ authoring environment.
Users describe formatting and editing intentions in natural language: drafting document structure, arranging tables, inserting figures, or resolving compilation problems. The underlying \LaTeX\ markup is handled by the application, allowing users who are unfamiliar with \LaTeX\ to concentrate on their own material.

Three components make up Spartan Write.
The Tauri/React frontend provides chat, preview, source editing, accessibility controls, and project management.
On the user's machine, a local FastAPI sidecar manages filesystem operations, compilation, and image processing.
In the cloud, a LangGraph agent coordinates document edits while enforcing ethical-use constraints.
Evaluation is carried out with a reproducible benchmark suite that compares LLM endpoints across task completion, token use, duration, cost, and tool-use behaviour.
Across 119 model–task pairs, the benchmark records a 98.3\% completion rate.
Provider costs vary by nearly two orders of magnitude, and much of the observed difference in latency and cost can be traced to how each model invokes tools.

On these metrics, Google's Gemini 3 Flash emerges as the recommended default model. It completes all compatible tasks, records the lowest average duration (12.9\,s), and achieves the second-lowest mean cost (\$0.0094 per run).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Researchers drafting theses, chapters, reports, or papers often turn to \LaTeX, a typesetting system widely used for technical and scientific documents \cite{latex}.
By separating an author's content from the rules that govern layout and formatting, \LaTeX\ allows publishers and editors to enforce visual consistency without rewriting the text itself.

That separation is useful institutionally, but demanding for authors.
First-time and occasional users must manage markup while also developing the intellectual content of their work.

Meanwhile, large language models (LLMs) and generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) have become powerful productivity tools across many domains.
Academic writing raises a more difficult set of questions.
Scholars are rightly concerned that GenAI tools may compromise academic integrity through inadvertent plagiarism, fabricated citations, or unsupported claims.

This report introduces Spartan Write, an AI-powered document authoring platform for scholars preparing reports, articles, papers, and theses.
The system is designed around a narrower and more defensible role for AI in academic work: assistance with technical writing infrastructure rather than the production of scholarly claims.
Its guiding principle is simple: \textbf{the tool only works with content that the author provides}. It does not generate, hallucinate, or synthesise new claims, arguments, or evidence.
Instead, Spartan Write concentrates on the technical and structural labour of academic writing: formatting, organisation, and presentation.

At its core, Spartan Write pairs an intelligent agent with \LaTeX\ so that users can revise documents through ordinary conversation.
Tables, figure placement, captions, \LaTeX\ formatting, and organisation of user-provided material can all be handled without requiring authors to edit raw markup.
The agent is explicitly constrained not to generate new substantive content.
Users supply the material; the agent translates it into appropriate academic structure and formatting.
Hand-curated templates cover common document types, including thesis reports and journal papers.
The intended result is a writing environment in which researchers retain control over their ideas while delegating technical document work to the application.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{\LaTeX\ authoring and usability}

\LaTeX\ gives authors substantial control over technical and scientific documents, but that control depends on familiarity with the language's syntax and semantics.
Experienced users can become highly efficient; first-time or one-time users often spend disproportionate effort on the mechanics of the system.
Prior work shows that \LaTeX\ is especially strong for mathematical expressions and equations, a strength recognised by both experienced and inexperienced users \cite{knauff_efficiency_2014}.
General-purpose document processors such as Microsoft Word, Google Docs, and LibreOffice acknowledge this value by allowing authors to embed \LaTeX\ snippets for mathematical notation \cite{matthews_craft_2019}.
Tables remain much harder: their syntax is verbose, visually unintuitive, and difficult even for experienced users to render correctly \cite{knauff_efficiency_2014}.

\subsection{Automated \LaTeX\ generation}

The complexity of \LaTeX\ syntax follows from the degree of detail and control it offers. In practice, however, even simple document elements can be difficult to produce.
Several research efforts therefore try to reduce the need to write \LaTeX\ code directly.
For table creation, Kayal et al. developed a system that recognises tables using OCR and renders them with a ResNet transformer \cite{kayal_tables_2023}.
Xia et al. pursued a related approach for converting images and tables into \LaTeX-compatible code \cite{xia_latexnet_2025}. Using images, tables, and corresponding \LaTeX\ code collected from the preprint repository ArXiv, they trained an image-multiplexer convolutional neural network.
The generated code showed high similarity to the expected output.

OpenAI's Prism offers an interaction model that closely resembles Spartan Write \cite{openai_prism}.
Researchers can edit documents through natural language while the tool maintains the underlying \LaTeX\ source internally.
Its ethical framing is the important difference: Prism proposes a model in which authors claim ownership over AI-generated content.
As discussed below, academic communities remain cautious, and often openly resistant, toward generated content in research publications.
Spartan Write therefore adopts a more restrictive position, preserving the benefits of AI assistance while keeping substantive authorship with the researcher.

Related tools are also emerging in this problem space.
OpenPrism \cite{github_openprism} is an open-source effort to recreate Prism-like functionality.
Bibby AI \cite{bibby_ai} generates code from natural language requests, while Autype reduces direct \LaTeX\ use through a markdown-like syntax \cite{autype}.
These tools make \LaTeX\ authoring more approachable, but they do not fully resolve the ethical distinction between formatting assistance and generated academic content.

\subsection{Generative AI in academic writing}

LLMs and GenAI have been adopted quickly in creative work, software development, data analysis, and other fields.
Academic writing remains more contested.
Students and researchers are commonly warned against uncritical use; recent studies associate increased LLM usage in publications with lower material quality, communication inaccuracies, and unusual shifts in language and vocabulary \cite{kobak_delving_2025}.
Survey evidence similarly suggests that GenAI remains widely distrusted in academic settings \cite{kwon_survey_2025}.
Researchers in STEM-adjacent fields may be more willing to accept disclosed use of GenAI, while scholars in non-STEM fields tend to be more restrictive about its role in published literature.

PaperDebugger \cite{paperdebugger} extends Overleaf, the widely used online \LaTeX\ editor, through a plugin-based multi-agent system.
It supports common paper-writing tasks such as reviewing, editing, and document repair.
Repository metrics suggest that the tool has attracted meaningful practical use.

A review of eleven studies published between 2021 and 2023 identifies benefits in areas such as "academic text generation, plagiarism detection, and language learning support" \cite{guo_review_2024}.
Those benefits are tempered by concerns around integrity, accuracy, plagiarism, and over-reliance.

Another survey, drawing on 24 studies, identifies several ways AI can support academic writing: idea generation, content structuring, literature review, data management and analysis, and proofreading.
Here too, the benefits are paired with concerns about integrity.

The emerging consensus is not that AI has no place in academic writing, but that its role must be carefully bounded.
Used within academic norms, AI can improve quality, efficiency, and productivity without replacing the author's intellectual contribution.

\subsection{Other tools}

General agentic environments such as Cursor, Copilot, Claude, or OpenAI Codex can also be used to edit \LaTeX\ documents.
Spartan Write differs from them through its narrower design.
It is purpose-built for academic writing rather than general software or text editing.
Hand-curated templates provide consistent starting points, avoiding ad hoc generated templates.
Because the agent is tightly connected to the compiler and ships with the rest of the application, compilation warnings and failures can be surfaced immediately and corrected in context.
A dedicated framework also makes it possible to ship product updates and ethical safeguards as part of the same workflow.
\LaTeX\ was adopted by the academic community for consistency and reliability; Spartan Write aims to preserve those qualities while reducing the burden placed on authors.

\section{Problem statement}

Preparing academic documents remains a persistent bottleneck for researchers and students.
\LaTeX\ offers exceptional quality and control, but fluency requires authors to learn conventions of layout, structure, and syntax.
Writers must move constantly between content development and markup management, dividing attention between the argument they want to make and the technical form required to express it.
Even experienced users spend time on repetitive tasks such as organising sections, placing and captioning figures, managing bibliography references, and correcting formatting inconsistencies.
These inefficiencies also affect the publishing pipeline.
Publishers and editors may spend hours exchanging corrections with authors over formatting errors, misaligned figures, and citation inconsistencies that better tooling could have prevented.
Word processors such as Microsoft Word and Google Docs are easier to learn, but they rarely provide the precision, scalability, and polished output required for academic publishing.
Researchers therefore face an unsatisfying choice: invest significant time in mastering \LaTeX, or accept lower-quality documents with fewer technical affordances.
By automating the technical side of \LaTeX\ authoring through an AI assistant, Spartan Write helps newcomers and students produce publication-ready documents without first mastering the full syntax of the system.

\subsection{Functional requirements}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Create \LaTeX\ projects from pre-defined templates.
    \item Open \LaTeX\ projects downloaded from other publishers.
    \item Converse with an AI agent that manages the document's source code.
    \item Allow direct source code manipulation if desired by the user.
    \item Support image uploads to the agent to attach to the document, or to parse and generate \LaTeX\ code, based on the user's request.
    \item Evaluate and configure the best LLM and provider for the task using a reproducible test suite.
    \item Deploy a user guide for grad-level students to quickly onboard and get started.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Non-functional requirements}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Track AI usage and establish limits on usage using user accounts.
    \item Allow bring-your-own-key (BYOK) users to bypass predefined limits at their expense.
    \item Establish safeguards to ensure the agent remains within its scope.
    \item Design the agent to adhere to constraints of ethical use.
    \item Collect usage statistics for product iteration and insights.
    \item Split the application architecture in order to establish separation of concerns.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Frontend}

Spartan Write's frontend is built with React.js \cite{react} on Tauri, allowing a single TypeScript/React codebase to produce native desktop applications for macOS, Windows, and Linux \cite{tauri}.

\subsubsection{Development tooling and libraries}

The frontend uses modern development tooling to support maintainability, accessibility, and a responsive editing experience.

\begin{itemize}
    \item Tauri provides wrappers for native system APIs while minimising binary size, making it well suited to deployment across major desktop environments \cite{tauri}.
    \item Vite enables fast build times, hot module reloading during development, and a lightweight packaging pipeline \cite{vite}.
    \item TypeScript provides type safety and supports maintainable frontend development \cite{typescript}.
    \item Styling is handled by Tailwind CSS for responsive, utility-first design \cite{tailwind}.
    \item User interface components are derived from shadcn, a plug-and-play component library built with accessibility, extensibility, and consistent visual design in mind \cite{shadcn}.
    \item The source code editor implements the Monaco Editor, which provides syntax-aware code editing with support for \LaTeX\ and other file formats \cite{monaco}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Agentic AI chat integration}

A central frontend feature is its integration with CopilotKit, which embeds agentic chat directly inside the editor \cite{copilotkit}.
The chat system supports tool use for file operations, \LaTeX\ compilation, and image handling.
This integration allows users to request document modifications conversationally without leaving the workspace.

\subsubsection{Dual-mode editor layout}

The editor uses a resizable split-panel layout with two complementary viewing modes.
In \emph{source view}, users work with a syntax-highlighted code editor and a file browser for project navigation.
In the default \emph{preview view}, users see a live PDF rendering of the compiled document.
Together, these modes provide immediate visual feedback and help users verify that agent-assisted edits produce the intended result.

\subsubsection{Accessible interface}

Accessibility is treated as a core design requirement rather than an optional enhancement.
The interface includes configurable text sizing, screen reader support, and a magnifier mode.
The UI is built from industry-standard components designed with accessibility in mind.

\subsubsection{Authentication}

The frontend integrates WorkOS AuthKit to provide secure, standards-based authentication \cite{workos}.
The authentication framework balances security with developer and user convenience.
Once authenticated, the frontend manages user credentials and displays usage data and cost information.

\subsubsection{Project management}

Project management is handled in the frontend as well.
From the dashboard, users can view and open recent projects, follow the first-run onboarding flow, and review profile and usage information in settings.

\subsection{Sidecar}

Bundled with the desktop application, the sidecar is a local FastAPI server \cite{fastapi} that connects the frontend UI to the project filesystem.
Unlike a conventional remote server, it runs on the user's device alongside the client application \cite{tauri_sidecar}.
Its main responsibility is local filesystem work, which keeps the cloud service stateless and focused on AI-agent logic.

\subsubsection{Template discovery}

Template discovery is exposed through a sidecar endpoint that lists available project templates.
Because the frontend reads from that endpoint, new templates can be added to the sidecar without requiring frontend changes.

\subsubsection{Project and file management}

Project lifecycle management also happens locally.
When a user selects a template, the sidecar scaffolds the associated directory structure and source files.
Endpoints for reading, writing, deleting, and renaming files operate within the project directory.

\subsubsection{Compilation and PDF rendering}

Local \LaTeX\ compilation is one of the sidecar's most important functions.
Tectonic \cite{tectonic}, a lightweight \LaTeX\ compiler, builds the project.
The compiler is wrapped behind an API that returns compilation status and error messages to the frontend.
After successful compilation, the generated PDF is served back to the frontend for live preview.
The Tectonic binary is bundled with the sidecar as part of the desktop application \cite{tauri_sidecar}.
This local compilation strategy supports fast iteration without depending on cloud-based typesetting services.


\subsubsection{Agent tool system}

Document manipulation occurs through sidecar tools for reading, writing, deleting, and renaming files; listing project contents; compiling \LaTeX; forwarding compilation errors; and managing attached images.
All file operations include path validation to prevent traversal attacks and keep modifications inside the project directory.
This toolset allows the agent to update project files in response to user requests while preserving local security boundaries.

\subsubsection{Image processing}

Images follow one of two paths.
An image may need to be inserted into the document, or it may need to be interpreted so the agent can answer a question or make an editing decision.
For insertion, the sidecar copies the image into the project directory and the agent adds the necessary \LaTeX\ reference.
For interpretation, the image can be processed without being stored in the project.
Both workflows are supported.

\subsubsection{Settings, health, and lifecycle}

Project-specific and user-level configuration is also managed by the sidecar, including user information, authentication tokens, and API credentials for users who provide their own keys.
These settings are persisted locally for quick access and offline use.
To conserve resources, the sidecar monitors its parent process: if the desktop application terminates, the sidecar exits automatically and avoids leaving orphaned processes.
In effect, the sidecar contains the local, filesystem-bound responsibilities of the application: compilation, configuration, and project-file management.

\subsection{Agent}

The cloud-based agent interprets user requests and coordinates document edits.
Built on Python FastAPI \cite{fastapi} and powered by LangGraph \cite{langgraph}, it manages multi-turn conversations, invokes document-manipulation tools, and maintains strict security boundaries.
OpenRouter provides flexible language-model selection \cite{openrouter}; WorkOS authentication validates user actions \cite{workos}; and streamed responses keep the frontend updated while usage is tracked for billing and analytics.

\subsubsection{Cloud deployment}

Deployment uses a containerised agent running on a cloud virtual machine.
Because the service is stateless between requests, it can scale independently and run across multiple instances with relatively little coordination.

\subsubsection{Agent behavior \& provision}

LangGraph defines the agent's behaviour as a stateful, multi-step workflow that preserves conversation context while executing editing logic in a controlled graph \cite{langgraph}.
Rather than binding Spartan Write to a single provider, OpenRouter supplies a configurable LLM aggregation layer for selecting models by cost, performance, and capability requirements \cite{openrouter}.

\subsubsection{Authentication}

Before processing any request, the agent validates user authorisation by verifying the bearer token against WorkOS \cite{workos}.
Only authenticated users are permitted to invoke the agent.

\subsubsection{Analytics \& privacy}

For usage analytics, billing, and performance insight, agent interactions are reported to PostHog \cite{posthog}. The agent supports a "bring your own key" (BYOK) model in which users provide API credentials per request, without persistent storage.
Conversation history is maintained within a single session to support multi-turn coherence. To protect privacy, chats are not persisted across separate sessions.

\subsection{Benchmark}

For evaluation, the benchmark module assesses the agent across document-editing scenarios.
It executes a test dataset against the agent API and scores the results using several metrics, producing evidence about capability, bottlenecks, and differences across models and configurations.
Its practical goal is to select a feasible default language model and monitor performance as providers update their models.

\subsubsection{Testing workflow}

During testing, the benchmark module interacts with the agent server through its REST API and runs a command-line evaluation pipeline.
It sends test prompts, captures responses and tool invocations, and generates timestamped results organised by model.

\subsubsection{Curated test suite}

The curated test suite pairs document states with user prompts.
Each dataset item represents a specific editing task, allowing systematic evaluation across a range of realistic scenarios.
Metadata records the model, task category, and task complexity for later analysis.

\subsubsection{Performance analysis}

Each run is scored with both objective and subjective metrics.
Objective measures cover token consumption, tool-use counts, and chat duration.
Subjective evaluation assesses output quality, edit correctness, and adherence to \LaTeX\ conventions.
Because the scoring framework is modular, new metrics can be added without changing the core evaluation logic.

\subsubsection{Visualization of results}

Evaluation results are visualised in a dashboard that aggregates scores across test cases and supports drill-down analysis of individual runs.
This interactive interface helps identify performance shifts, failure modes, and differences across models and configurations.

\subsubsection{Reproducibility}

Maintaining the test dataset and deterministic scoring logic enables reproducible testing across model versions, providers, and configurations.
This reproducibility is essential for informed model selection.
It supports confident comparison before deployment.
The standardised evaluation approach also supports tracking improvements over time as the agent evolves.

\subsection{User guide}

MkDocs powers the user guide, producing a clean, searchable website from Markdown files.
The guide acts as the single source of truth for user-facing documentation across supported platforms, with built-in search, responsive design, and clear navigation.
Its content spans the full user journey: platform-specific installation for macOS, Windows, and Linux; quick-start tutorials; project management; API configuration; tips for efficient agent use; best practices; and troubleshooting.
With this coverage, users can find help at each stage of their experience with Spartan Write.

\subsection{Build pipeline}

Packaging Spartan Write requires several build stages, since each component must be compiled and bundled before release.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The sidecar is compiled and packaged into a standalone binary using PyInstaller.
\item Next, the Tectonic binary for \LaTeX\ compilation is downloaded from its repository.
\item Both binaries are copied into the Tauri source code and registered for bundling.
\item The Tauri frontend is compiled with the bundled binaries to create an installable package.
\item These steps are repeated on Windows, macOS, and Ubuntu to generate installable packages for each operating system.
\item In parallel, the server is built and packaged as a Docker image, ready to be pulled and deployed to a server.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Agent design}

Implementation of the agent uses a LangGraph state machine with a deterministic execution graph \cite{langgraph}.
A comprehensive system prompt defines its role as an agentic assistant for interpreting user requests and making document modifications.
The prompt specifies the workflow (list files $\rightarrow$ read $\rightarrow$ edit $\rightarrow$ compile), file-organisation conventions, and safety principles.

\subsubsection{Tool invocation flow}

When action is needed, the agent invokes the appropriate tool and receives structured output such as success status, file paths, and error messages.
Tool results return to the agent loop, allowing it to respond to outcomes, handle errors, or ask the user for clarification.

A typical user request is handled as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The agent reads the user request and uses \texttt{list\_files\_tool} to understand the project structure.
\item It reads \texttt{main.tex}, which indicates the layout of the document.
\item Based on the request, it reads the section or sections needed for the task.
\item If there is an image, the appropriate tool imports the image into the project.
\item Requested changes are written to the relevant files.
\item The compilation tool is invoked to check for errors. If compilation fails, the agent repeats the process to resolve the issue.
\item Finally, the agent responds with a summary of its actions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Safety \& ethical constraints}

Several built-in mechanisms constrain agent behaviour.

\begin{itemize}
\item All file operations validate paths at the tooling level to prevent escaping the project directory.
\item The prompt instructs the agent not to add unsolicited content.
\item Compilation errors trigger error feedback instead of silent failures.
\end{itemize}
Together, these constraints position the agent as a trusted scribe rather than an unconstrained autonomous writer.

\subsection{\LaTeX\ compilation}

The \LaTeX\ compilation subsystem is implemented as part of the sidecar \cite{tauri_sidecar}.
Tectonic is the core typesetting backend \cite{tectonic}.

Client requests include the target project directory and invoke Tectonic through subprocess execution with configurable parameters.
Both standard output and error streams are captured for diagnostics.
When compilation succeeds, the output PDF path is returned to the user.

Compilation can be initiated by either the user or the agent.
When the agent initiates compilation, status and error messages provide the feedback needed to debug and repair the document.

\subsection{User guide}

The user guide is a lightweight static website for getting started with Spartan Write.
It includes step-by-step material on installation, usage, troubleshooting, and uninstallation.
MkDocs converts the Markdown source into web pages \cite{mkdocs}.

\subsection{Benchmark}

The benchmark module evaluates language models through the Spartan Write agent.
Its reproducible test suite combines test documents, user requests, and scoring metrics.
Four core components make up the module:
\begin{itemize}
\item a CLI runner for orchestration;
\item a scoring engine for metric evaluation;
\item SQLite database storage for persistence;
\item and a Streamlit dashboard for result visualisation.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Flow}

Execution follows a defined pipeline.
\begin{itemize}
\item The dataset is loaded into the result directory and the database is initialised.
\item For each test case, a workspace directory is prepared and the prompt is sent to the agent server.
\item After the chat completes, the scoring engine extracts tool usage, duration, and token-consumption metrics.
\item These metrics are stored in the database.
\item The dashboard then aggregates results from the database and renders interactive visualisations.
\end{itemize}

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Document structure manipulation} \\

Tests 000, 001, 002, 003, 004, 006, 010, and 011 validate the agent's ability to create, modify, and reorganise \LaTeX\ documents.
They ask whether the model can parse document hierarchy, create sections, insert or remove content, and reorder structural elements.
Failures in this category would indicate difficulty with parent-child relationships in \LaTeX\ or with preserving document structure.
This group establishes the baseline functionality expected of the agent.

\item \textbf{Search and targeted deletion} \\

Tests 007, 008, and 009 assess whether long-document content can be located and removed under both explicit and semantically indirect instructions.
Two related abilities are measured: precise deletion when the target text is specified, and contextual deletion when a topic appears in varied wording across the document.
Failure here would suggest weak contextual recognition or insufficient editing precision.

\item \textbf{Document understanding and synthesis} \\
Tests 005 and 012 examine document-level understanding and synthesis.
In Test 005, the model must distil a large document into a clear summary.
In Test 012, it must generate new material that remains within the style and scope of the existing document.
Weak performance would suggest difficulty maintaining coherence across long documents or aligning new text with established context.
\item \textbf{Error recovery and problem diagnosis} \\
Tests 014 and 015 evaluate whether the model can identify and repair broken \LaTeX\ documents under different instruction styles.
One test gives a direct repair instruction: "Fix the document."
The other is less explicit and expects the model to infer that repair is needed.
Together, these tests assess diagnostic ability, responsiveness to implicit cues, and practical \LaTeX\ knowledge.
Failure would suggest that the model misses errors, misreads user intent, or lacks sufficient command of \LaTeX\ repair patterns.
\item \textbf{Multimodal integration} \\
Tests 013 and 016 evaluate multimodal document editing.
The first checks whether an image can be inserted at the correct location with an appropriate file reference.
The second asks for a description of an attached image.
Weak performance may reflect limitations in image understanding, file-reference generation, or document placement.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Scoring metrics}

Three primary metrics are computed for each benchmark run.
\textbf{Tool use} aggregates tool calls by name and counts invocations per tool type, showing which capabilities the model relied on.
\textbf{Chat duration} records the time required for the agent to complete the task, helping identify performance degradation after model updates or provider outages.
\textbf{Token usage} sums input tokens, output tokens, and reasoning tokens across all AI messages to track resource consumption.

Filtering is available by model, execution status, error presence, and keyword search across job IDs and test summaries, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:bench-dashboard}.
Plotly charts \cite{plotly} compare scores across models and display score distributions for closer analysis.
Run-level views expose error messages, chat results, and individual metrics.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Evaluation questions}

The benchmark test suite described in Section 4 was run on seven large-language-model endpoints: \texttt{anthropic/claude-opus-4.6}, \texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.6}, \texttt{openai/gpt-5.4}, \texttt{openai/gpt-5.4-mini}, \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-v3.2}, \texttt{google/gemini-3-flash-preview}, and \texttt{x-ai/grok-4-fast}.
Each of the seventeen tests was executed up to three times per endpoint.
Together, the reported metrics and visualisations answer the following questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reliability.} Do the evaluated models complete the tasks without erroring, and are any failures concentrated on a particular model or task class?
    \item \textbf{Task difficulty.} Which benchmark tasks are intrinsically hard, independent of the model used?
    \item \textbf{Resource usage.} How much context and how many output tokens does each model consume on average?
    \item \textbf{Efficiency.} Does wall-clock duration scale predictably with token volume, or do some models exhibit anomalous latency?
    \item \textbf{Cost.} What is the monetary cost per completed run, and how large is the spread between providers?
    \item \textbf{Per-task model comparison.} Are the slowdowns observed for any model concentrated on a particular subset of tasks?
    \item \textbf{Agent behaviour.} Do the models exhibit distinguishable tool-use patterns that can explain their differences in cost and duration?
\end{enumerate}

Three behavioural patterns are visible.
DeepSeek issues the largest number of \texttt{read\_file} invocations, which explains the elevated input-token average reported in Table~\ref{tab:token-averages}.
Its slowness and token consumption therefore share a root cause in the agent loop.
Gemini~3~Flash reads files comparatively little but renames them frequently, suggesting a restructure-in-place strategy that differs from the read-then-edit pattern used by other endpoints.
Neither \texttt{gpt-5.4} nor \texttt{gpt-5.4-mini} invokes \texttt{rename\_file} at all, even though the suite includes tasks where renaming is a natural solution path. This should be investigated at the prompt or tool-schema level.
For question~(7), much of the observed cost and duration variation can be attributed to concrete, model-specific tool-use behaviour rather than raw model speed alone.

\subsection{Summary of findings}

Taken together, the dashboard supports five concrete findings.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Task-level reliability is high, and every failure is attributable to a single provider's lack of multimodal support.
    The failures therefore isolate a capability gap rather than a systemic issue.

    \item Task difficulty is dominated by large-document structural edits, independently of the model used. All compatible models are capable of completing the full non-multimodal task set.

    \item Cost varies by nearly two orders of magnitude across providers for similar completion behaviour, with Grok~4~Fast and Gemini~3~Flash at the lower end and the Claude family at the higher end.

    \item Tool-usage patterns, rather than raw model speed alone, explain much of the observed cost and latency variance.
    With $5.08$ \texttt{read\_file} invocations per run, the highest of any model, DeepSeek directly increases both its $62{,}485$-token input average and its $148.6$\,s mean duration, because redundant reads reinsert file contents into the prompt on later turns.

    Gemini~3~Flash exhibits the opposite profile: the lowest \texttt{read\_file} count ($1.65$) paired with the highest \texttt{rename\_file} count ($3.83$). This pattern suggests a restructure-in-place strategy that plausibly helps explain its position as the fastest and one of the cheapest endpoints in the study.

    \item Tool-level interventions are a promising direction for future work. The \texttt{gpt-5.4} family never invokes \texttt{rename\_file} across any of its $102$ completed runs, despite several tasks admitting a rename-based solution. The cause is not yet clear, but the pattern suggests a possible mismatch between GPT models and the current tool schema.
\end{enumerate}

On the basis of the metrics collected in this study, the recommended default model for the agent is \texttt{google/gemini-3-flash-preview}.
It combines a $100\%$ task-level completion rate on the compatible portion of the suite with the lowest mean chat duration ($12.9$\,s), the second-lowest mean cost ($\$0.0094$ per run), and a tool-use pattern well matched to document-scale editing tasks.

\texttt{x-ai/grok-4-fast} is a credible secondary choice when cost is the overriding concern: at $\$0.0032$ per run, it is roughly $3\times$ cheaper, though its slightly longer mean duration ($16.4$\,s) makes it marginally less suited to an interactive chat experience.

The Anthropic models are functionally reliable, but they are between one and two orders of magnitude more expensive per run than these alternatives.
For this workload, they are difficult to justify unless future quality metrics show a correspondingly large improvement in output correctness.

DeepSeek~v3.2 is not recommended for this configuration because it lacks multimodal support and shows redundant read behaviour on large-document tasks.

\section{Conclusion}

This report introduced Spartan Write, an agentic AI system for academic document authoring.
The discussion began from two practical constraints: general-purpose word processors lack the precision expected in academic publishing, while \LaTeX{}-based environments such as Overleaf impose a steep learning curve.

\subsection{Key findings and interpretation}
The benchmark reveals an important pattern: differences in cost and latency among modern LLMs depend less on raw model speed alone than on how models use tools.

All capable models complete tasks at roughly the same rate (Table~\ref{tab:task-completion}), but they reach that outcome through different strategies.
DeepSeek repeatedly reads files that need not be reloaded, pushing its token use to $62{,}485$ per run, or $3.6\times$ the amount used by GPT-5.4 (Table~\ref{tab:token-averages}), and producing a mean duration of $148.6$\,s (Figure~\ref{fig:bench-duration-vs-tokens}).
This appears less like a reasoning failure than a consequence of tool-invocation habits that reinsert file contents into context on each turn.
Gemini~3~Flash, by contrast, appears to restructure in place using \texttt{rename\_file} (Figure~\ref{fig:bench-tool-use}) rather than repeatedly reading and editing. That strategy aligns with its lowest observed latency ($12.9$\,s) and second-lowest cost ($\$0.0094$ per run, Figure~\ref{fig:bench-cost-per-model}).

Two takeaways follow from this finding.
For document-editing workloads, models should be evaluated not only by completion rate but also by whether their tool use matches efficient editing patterns.
Prompt engineering and tool-schema design may also shift a model from an expensive outlier to a competitive option.
The GPT family never uses \texttt{rename\_file} in any of its $102$ completed runs (Figure~\ref{fig:bench-tool-use}), even when rename-based solutions are available. This pattern suggests a schema-level mismatch rather than a lack of reasoning ability.

Across all models, the hardest tasks involve large-document structural changes (Table~\ref{tab:hardest-tasks}, Figure~\ref{fig:bench-duration-by-job}) rather than weaknesses tied to a specific provider.
This suggests that the main complexity of document editing lies in the work itself: preserving structure, context, and intent across long files.
Future improvements should therefore focus on better support for large-context document operations, rather than treating each model's behaviour as an isolated problem.

On the basis of these findings, \texttt{google/gemini-3-flash-preview} is the recommended primary model for Spartan Write.
It is the fastest ($12.9$\,s), among the least expensive ($\$0.0094$ per run), and well aligned with document-editing workflows.
The recommendation, however, rests on efficiency rather than correctness. Completing a task quickly and cheaply does not necessarily mean that the resulting edit is the best one.

\subsection{Contributions}

This work makes two contributions to LLM-driven document authoring.

First, it presents Spartan Write, a document authoring system that shows how agentic AI can reduce cognitive load in \LaTeX{}-based technical writing.
Rather than requiring users to master tool-specific syntax for editing, the system abstracts file operations and document structure behind a natural-language interface, allowing users to express intent while the agent handles implementation details.

Second, it contributes a benchmark methodology for evaluating LLMs on document-editing tasks.
The suite includes 17 parameterised test cases covering single-edit operations, large-scale refactoring, error correction, and multimodal tasks.
It reports metrics for reliability, latency, token usage, cost, and tool-invocation patterns.

\subsection{Limitations and future work}

One key limitation should be emphasised: this benchmark measures \textbf{efficiency} rather than output \textbf{correctness}.
Although the benchmark reports task completion and performance metrics, "completion" only means that the agent finished without a runtime error.
It does not guarantee that the edit matches the user's intent or that the document remains semantically correct.
The recommendation for Gemini is therefore based on efficiency and assumes, for the purposes of comparison, that all models produce equally correct outputs.

Substantial variation in output quality would make the efficiency rankings incomplete.
In practice, a slightly slower but more reliable model may be preferable if it produces more accurate edits.
A fuller evaluation should specify correctness expectations for each task.
For example, the content-generation-resistance test could assert that the write tool is never called.
Other approaches include comparing edits against reference versions, using structured human annotation, or asking an LLM judge to evaluate a sample of benchmark runs.
Adding these quality metrics is the main priority for future work because it would provide a stronger basis for model selection.

Beyond quality assessment, two secondary research directions remain: investigating the GPT family's systematic avoidance of \texttt{rename\_file}, and validating the benchmark against real-world use through human-centred studies of task completion time, error rates, and user satisfaction.

Several Spartan Write UX enhancements also merit exploration, including undo support for agent actions, real-time multi-user collaboration, and SyncTeX integration, which would allow the agent to map PDF coordinates back to source locations during edits.
