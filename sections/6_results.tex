\section{Results}

\subsection{Evaluation questions}

The benchmark test suite described in Section 4 was run against a suite of seventeen tests, each executed up to three times, on seven large-language-model endpoints: \texttt{anthropic/claude-opus-4.6}, \texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.6}, \texttt{openai/gpt-5.4}, \texttt{openai/gpt-5.4-mini}, \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-v3.2}, \texttt{google/gemini-3-flash-preview}, and \texttt{x-ai/grok-4-fast}.
Taken together, the reported metrics and visualisations are designed to answer the following questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Reliability.} Do the evaluated models complete the tasks without erroring, and are any failures concentrated on a particular model or task class?
    \item \textbf{Task difficulty.} Which benchmark tasks are intrinsically hard, independent of the model used?
    \item \textbf{Resource usage.} How much context and how many output tokens does each model consume on average?
    \item \textbf{Efficiency.} Does wall-clock duration scale predictably with token volume, or do some models exhibit anomalous latency?
    \item \textbf{Cost.} What is the monetary cost per completed run, and how large is the spread between providers?
    \item \textbf{Per-task model comparison.} Are the slowdowns observed for any model concentrated on a particular subset of tasks?
    \item \textbf{Agent behaviour.} Do the models exhibit distinguishable tool-use patterns that can explain their differences in cost and duration?
\end{enumerate}

The apparent $2.9$\,s mean for \texttt{016-large-parse-image} is an artefact of DeepSeek aborting early and should be considered alongside the data from Figure~\ref{fig:bench-run-status}.
This answers question~(2): task difficulty on the suite is dominated by document-scale restructuring and repair, and the suite contains tests encompassing a distribution of easy to difficult tasks.

\subsection{Duration versus token volume}

Figure~\ref{fig:bench-duration-vs-tokens} plots each run's duration against its total token count, categorized by language model.
The expected roughly linear relationship between workload size and elapsed time is present for the OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, and Grok endpoints.
DeepSeek occupies the upper-right of the plot, with a mean of $148.6$\,s per run, and is a clear outlier on both axes.
The scatter also supports identifying individual runs that deviate from the overall trend for reasons beyond token count, such as provider-side latency or inefficient tool invocation.
This answers question~(4): the duration is predictable from token volume for six of the seven models, and DeepSeek appears to be slow even after controlling for the larger contexts it assembles.

\subsection{Per-task model comparison}

Figure~\ref{fig:bench-duration-modelwise} projects the duration data as a grouped bar chart, breaking each task down by model.
This exposes task-specific regressions that a single overall mean would conceal.
Three observations emerge:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Gemini~3~Flash is consistently the fastest endpoint across nearly every task;
    \item DeepSeek is between 3-10 times slower than the remaining models on every large-document task;
    \item The \texttt{gpt-5.4} and \texttt{gpt-5.4-mini} endpoints record very similar durations across the suite, despite \texttt{mini} consuming more input tokens on average.
\end{itemize}
This implies that on this workload, the smaller model is cheaper but not meaningfully faster. This answers question~(6).

\subsection{Tool invocation patterns}
\label{sec:bench-tools}

The benchmark records the number of times each tool is invoked during a conversation.
Figure~\ref{fig:bench-tool-use} renders a model-by-tool heatmap, in which each cell shows the average number of invocations of a given tool per completed run.
High invocation counts may indicate thorough exploration or inefficient looping, depending on context.
The heatmap therefore complements rather than replaces the scoring discussion above.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/bench-tool-use.png}
    \caption{Tool patterns differ: DeepSeek re-reads excessively, Gemini restructures in-place, GPT avoids renames.}
    \label{fig:bench-tool-use}
\end{figure}

Three behavioural fingerprints are visible.
First, DeepSeek issues the largest number of \texttt{read\_file} invocations, which explains the elevated input-token average reported in Table~\ref{tab:token-averages}.
The model's slowness and its token appetite share a root cause in the agent loop.
Second, Gemini~3~Flash reads files comparatively little but renames them frequently, indicating a restructure-in-place strategy that differs qualitatively from the read-then-edit pattern used by the other endpoints. 
Third, neither \texttt{gpt-5.4} nor \texttt{gpt-5.4-mini} invokes \texttt{rename\_file} at all, despite the suite containing tasks for which renaming is a natural solution path; this is a candidate for prompt- or tool-schema-level investigation in future work.
This answers question~(7) and attributes a substantial fraction of the observed cost and duration differences to concrete, model-specific tool-use behaviour rather than to raw model speed.

\subsection{Summary of findings}

Taken together, the dashboard supports four concrete findings.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Task-level reliability is high and every failure is attributable to a single provider's lack of multimodal support.
    This isolates a capability gap rather than a systemic issue.
    
    \item Task difficulty is dominated by large-document structural edits, independently of the model used. All the compatible models are capable of addressing all of the tasks.
    
    \item Cost varies by nearly two orders of magnitude across providers for the same completion behaviour, with Grok~4~Fast and Gemini~3~Flash on the lower end, and the Claude family on the higher end.
    
    \item Tool-usage patterns, rather than raw model speed, explain most of
    the observed cost and latency variance.
    DeepSeek's $5.08$ \texttt{read\_file} invocations per run, which is the highest of any model, directly produce both its $62{,}485$-token input average and its $148.6$\,s mean duration, because every redundant read re-inserts file contents into the prompt on the next turn.
    
    Gemini~3~Flash exhibits the opposite profile: the lowest \texttt{read\_file} count ($1.65$) paired with the highest \texttt{rename\_file} count ($3.83$) implements a restructure-in-place strategy that plausibly accounts for its position as the fastest and one of the cheapest endpoints in the study.
    
    \item One significant tool-level interventions are candidates for future work. The \texttt{gpt-5.4} family never invokes \texttt{rename\_file} across any of its $102$ completed runs despite several tasks admitting a rename-based solution. It is not clear why this is the case, but this hints at an incompatibility with GPT models and the tooling schema.
\end{enumerate}

On the basis of the metrics collected in this study, the recommended default model for the agent is \texttt{google/gemini-3-flash-preview}.
It combines a $100\%$ task-level completion rate on the non-multimodal portion of the suite with the lowest mean chat duration ($12.9$\,s) and the second-lowest mean cost ($\$0.0094$ per run), and its tool-use strategy aligns well with the document-scale editing tasks that dominate the benchmark.

\texttt{x-ai/grok-4-fast} is a credible secondary choice where cost is the overriding concern: at $\$0.0032$ per run it is roughly $3\times$ cheaper again, though its slightly longer mean duration ($16.4$\,s) makes it marginally less suited to an interactive chat experience.

The Anthropic models, while functionally reliable, are between one and two orders of magnitude more expensive per run than these two alternatives and cannot be justified for this workload until a quality metric is added to the benchmark that demonstrates a correspondingly large improvement in output correctness.

DeepSeek~v3.2 is not recommended in any configuration, owing to both its missing multimodal support and its redundant read behaviour on large-document tasks.
